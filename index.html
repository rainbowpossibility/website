Mission

The Rainbow Possibility Foundation equips Los Angeles public high school classrooms with tangible STEAM materials so students can learn by doing.

We focus on real supplies students touch: soldering kits, sensors, wood and fasteners, adhesives, safety gear, and lab consumables. When classrooms have parts on hand, teachers can run labs on time, students can iterate safely, and learning becomes concrete and memorable.

What We Do

Supplies, not slogans. Hot glue, lab kits, electronics, wood, fasteners—real materials students use today.
Hands-on STEAM. Project-based learning where students design, test, and iterate.
Barrier-free classrooms. No canceled labs for lack of supplies.

Our purchasing approach is simple: prioritize items that unlock immediate instruction and give students multiple attempts. We avoid long delays and focus on what gets a lab or build running this week.

Our Approach to Learning

Students learn best by building and testing ideas with their own hands. We support teacher-designed activities that include prediction, trial, reflection, and revision. This cycle helps students develop persistence, attention to detail, and practical engineering judgment—all transferable skills for college and careers.

How We Choose

We prioritize items that: (1) enable whole-class participation, (2) allow iteration and safe retries, (3) align with current instruction so students use them now—not someday, and (4) are affordable and purchased with cost efficiency in mind. No waste.

Past Projects

Our purchases go directly to classroom builds—real materials for real student ideas. Projects change each semester as students generate new designs and challenges, but all follow the same goal—learn by doing with accessible, real materials.

  • Motors and magnets: Life-size student-built “Go-Fish” game modeled after the toddler magnetic toy, scaled up with working motors and custom catches.
  • Paint and color: Classroom-safe paints so students can personalize and decorate their finished creations.
  • Elastic tubing: Components for a student-made giant slingshot project exploring tension, energy storage, and launch mechanics.
  • 3-Phase Motor Controller: For student-engineered axial-flux motor builds testing torque, coil balance, and power delivery.
  • Prusa MK4S 3D Printer: High-reliability printer supporting rapid prototyping and iterative design in engineering classes.
  • PLA Filament: Ongoing stock for student prototypes and replacement parts across multiple engineering projects.
  • Wood and screws: Used for a three-person table maze that teaches cooperation, precision, and spatial reasoning.
  • Toothpicks, hot glue, and eggs: Supplies for the annual Physics Egg Drop challenge—students design and test impact-resistant structures using simple materials and real eggs.
  • Fabric and flexible materials: Used in a student-designed “fidget glove” exploring tactile design and wearable engineering.

Each project focuses on active experimentation, cost efficiency, and materials that students can touch, test, and refine—no waste, no idle supplies. Many materials are reused and repurposed from past builds, stretching every dollar and giving new students a fresh start with proven tools.

How It Helps Students

Labs run on time. Materials on hand prevent cancellations and lost instructional minutes.
Iterate & improve. Extra parts let students test, fail safely, fix, and try again.
Career skills. Real tools and workflows build confidence for internships and college labs.
